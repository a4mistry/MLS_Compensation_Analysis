02 — The Superstars

The 1% of the pitch

MLS runs a barbell economy. A small elite of Designated Players sits at the top, a vast base earns near the minimum, and the middle is thin.

The top 10% of earners take home of all the money in the league. The single highest-paid player alone earns — enough to fund a full roster of league-minimum players several times over.

03 — The Market by Role

Attack pays. Goalkeeping doesn't.

The transfer market prices creativity and goals. Attacking midfielders and wingers command the highest median pay; goalkeepers — however vital — are the cheapest specialists on the field.

4 · The cautionary tale: Atlanta United

Atlanta shows the ceiling of the superstar model — and its risk.

With the league's -largest payroll (), Atlanta funneled of it into attack — above the league's — behind a front three of Almirón, Miranchuk and Latte Lath that eats of the wage bill. To pay for it, they went bargain-basement everywhere else: just on goalkeeping (vs league-wide) and thin at the back. The result: a PPG side. Loading up on attackers is no guarantee — balance is what Atlanta traded away.

What predicts results better?

Each pay tier's correlation with results (PPG), goals scored, and goals conceded. For goals conceded, a negative bar is good (spending → fewer conceded).

The read: if anything the stars edge it — top-3 pay shows the stronger link, especially with goals scored — but with only 30 clubs none of these correlations is statistically robust, so treat it as a lean, not a law. What's clear is the other side: Real Salt Lake and Nashville win with a bargain supporting cast, so spending on the middle class is not the deciding factor.
